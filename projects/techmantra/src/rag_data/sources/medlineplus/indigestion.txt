OVERVIEW
Indigestion, also called dyspepsia or upset stomach, is discomfort or pain in the upper abdomen. Indigestion is not a disease, but rather some symptoms you experience, including abdominal pain and a feeling of fullness soon after you start eating. Indigestion can also be caused by other digestive conditions.

Most people experience occasional indigestion. For most people, it is not serious and goes away on its own. However, if indigestion is persistent or associated with other symptoms such as weight loss or blood in stool, it may indicate a more serious condition requiring medical evaluation.

SYMPTOMS
Common symptoms of indigestion include:
- Burning or discomfort in the upper abdomen or stomach (epigastric pain)
- Feeling full too soon while eating (early satiety)
- Uncomfortable fullness after a meal that lasts longer than it should
- Bloating — a feeling of tightness or fullness in the abdomen
- Nausea, sometimes with vomiting
- Belching and gas
- Acidic taste in the mouth
- Growling or gurgling stomach sounds
- Heartburn — burning sensation in the chest that occurs when stomach acid backs up into the esophagus

CAUSES
Indigestion can be caused by a variety of factors including:
- Eating too fast or too much
- Eating fatty, greasy, or spicy foods
- Drinking too much caffeine, alcohol, or carbonated beverages
- Smoking
- Anxiety and stress
- Certain medications including antibiotics, aspirin, and NSAIDs like ibuprofen and naproxen
- Swallowing too much air while eating
- Peptic ulcers
- Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD)
- Gastritis — inflammation of the stomach lining
- Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) infection
- Stomach cancer (rare)

DIAGNOSIS
Indigestion is usually diagnosed based on symptoms. If symptoms are persistent or concerning, your doctor may recommend:
- Blood tests to check for H. pylori infection or other abnormalities
- Breath test or stool test for H. pylori
- Upper endoscopy — a thin flexible tube with a camera is passed through the mouth into the stomach
- Abdominal ultrasound
- X-ray of the upper digestive system

TREATMENT
Lifestyle and dietary changes:
- Eat smaller meals more frequently throughout the day
- Eat slowly and chew food thoroughly
- Avoid foods that trigger your symptoms
- Avoid lying down immediately after eating — wait 2 to 3 hours
- Elevate the head of your bed if nighttime symptoms are a problem
- Reduce or eliminate alcohol and caffeine
- Stop smoking
- Manage stress through relaxation techniques

Over-the-counter medications:
- Antacids (Tums, Rolaids, Maalox) — neutralize stomach acid for quick relief
- H2 blockers (famotidine/Pepcid, cimetidine) — reduce acid production
- Proton pump inhibitors (omeprazole/Prilosec) — block acid production more effectively for frequent symptoms
- Simethicone (Gas-X) — helps relieve bloating and gas

Natural remedies:
- Ginger tea has anti-inflammatory properties and may help reduce nausea and indigestion
- Chamomile tea has a calming effect on the digestive system
- Peppermint may help reduce stomach spasms but can worsen GERD
- Licorice root (deglycyrrhizinated licorice or DGL) may help protect the stomach lining

WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR
Seek medical attention immediately if you experience:
- Chest pain, pressure, or tightness that may radiate to the jaw or left arm — this may be a heart attack
- Severe or persistent abdominal pain
- Vomiting blood or material that looks like coffee grounds
- Black, tarry, or bloody stools
- Difficulty swallowing or pain with swallowing
- Unexplained weight loss
- Loss of appetite that persists
- Frequent vomiting
- Jaundice — yellowing of the skin or eyes

CONTRAINDICATIONS AND WARNINGS
- Antacids containing calcium or magnesium can worsen kidney disease — consult a doctor if you have kidney problems
- Long-term use of NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) can cause or worsen peptic ulcers and gastritis
- Proton pump inhibitors should not be taken for more than 14 days without consulting a doctor
- If you are over age 55 and experiencing new or worsening symptoms, seek prompt medical evaluation

SPECIAL POPULATIONS
Pregnant women: Indigestion and heartburn are very common during pregnancy, especially in the third trimester. Antacids containing calcium are generally considered safe during pregnancy. Avoid sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) antacids.

People with diabetes: Some diabetes medications can cause nausea and indigestion as a side effect. Discuss this with your doctor.

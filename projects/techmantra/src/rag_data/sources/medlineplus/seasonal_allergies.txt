OVERVIEW
Hay fever, also called allergic rhinitis, causes cold-like signs and symptoms such as a runny nose, itchy eyes, congestion, sneezing and sinus pressure. But unlike a cold, hay fever is not caused by a virus. Hay fever is caused by an allergic response to outdoor or indoor allergens such as pollen, dust mites, or tiny flakes of skin and saliva shed by cats, dogs, and other animals with fur or feathers.

Hay fever can make you miserable and affect your performance at work or school and interfere with leisure activities. For some people, hay fever triggers or worsens asthma symptoms. Although hay fever is not caused by a virus, symptoms can be similar, making it hard to tell the difference at first.

SYMPTOMS
Seasonal allergy symptoms include:
- Runny nose with watery discharge
- Nasal congestion and stuffiness
- Sneezing, often repeatedly
- Itchy nose, eyes, ears, and throat
- Red, swollen, and watery eyes (allergic conjunctivitis)
- Postnasal drip — mucus running down the back of the throat
- Cough from postnasal drip
- Swollen, bluish-colored skin beneath the eyes (allergic shiners)
- Decreased sense of smell or taste
- Fatigue and general feeling of being unwell
- Headache from sinus congestion
- Mild sore throat

CAUSES AND TRIGGERS
During certain seasons, trees, grasses, and weeds release tiny pollen grains into the air. These pollen grains can enter your nose and trigger hay fever. Triggers include:
- Tree pollen — common in spring (oak, birch, cedar, alder)
- Grass pollen — common in late spring and summer (timothy, orchard, bluegrass)
- Ragweed and weed pollen — common in fall
- Mold spores — common in damp weather
- Dust mites and pet dander — year-round triggers

DIAGNOSIS
Your doctor may diagnose hay fever based on:
- A physical examination
- Your description of symptoms and when they occur
- Skin prick test — small amounts of allergens are scratched into the skin
- Allergy blood test (specific IgE test) — measures immune system response to specific allergens

TREATMENT AND MANAGEMENT
Antihistamines: Over-the-counter antihistamines help relieve sneezing, runny nose, and itchy or watery eyes. Non-drowsy options include loratadine (Claritin), cetirizine (Zyrtec), and fexofenadine (Allegra). Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) is effective but causes drowsiness.

Nasal corticosteroid sprays: These prescription-strength sprays are often the most effective medications for allergic rhinitis. Examples include fluticasone (Flonase) and triamcinolone (Nasacort), which are now available over the counter.

Decongestants: These medications can relieve stuffiness. Oral decongestants such as pseudoephedrine can raise blood pressure and are not recommended for people with hypertension.

Allergy shots (immunotherapy): For severe allergies, a doctor may recommend allergy shots. These shots gradually increase your exposure to allergens so your immune system becomes less sensitive to them over time.

SELF-CARE STEPS
- Monitor pollen counts using weather apps or websites and stay indoors on high-pollen days
- Keep windows and doors closed during pollen season
- Use air conditioning at home and in your car
- Wear sunglasses outdoors to protect your eyes
- Use a HEPA air purifier in your home
- Rinse your nasal passages with saline solution after outdoor exposure
- Shower and change clothes after spending time outdoors
- Avoid outdoor activity in the morning when pollen counts are highest
- Dry laundry indoors during allergy season rather than on a clothesline

WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR
Seek medical attention if:
- Your symptoms are severe and interfere with daily life
- Over-the-counter medications are not providing enough relief
- You also have asthma symptoms such as wheezing or shortness of breath
- You develop hives, significant skin rash, or swelling
- You experience difficulty breathing or swallowing (possible anaphylaxis — call 911 immediately)
- Symptoms persist year-round and are not responding to standard treatments

SPECIAL POPULATIONS
Children: Hay fever is one of the most common chronic conditions in children. It can affect school performance and quality of life. Consult a pediatrician before giving antihistamines to young children.

Pregnant women: Some antihistamines are considered safe during pregnancy, but always consult your doctor before taking any medication.

People with asthma: Hay fever can worsen asthma symptoms. Work with your doctor to manage both conditions together.

Source: MedlinePlus - Nausea and Vomiting
URL: https://medlineplus.gov/nauseaandvomiting.html

OVERVIEW:
Nausea is the unpleasant sensation of wanting to vomit. Vomiting is 
the forceful emptying of the stomach contents through the mouth. Both 
are symptoms of many different conditions and are usually not serious 
on their own.

COMMON CAUSES:
- Viral gastroenteritis (stomach flu) — most common cause
- Food poisoning
- Motion sickness
- Pregnancy (morning sickness)
- Medications including antibiotics, chemotherapy, opioids
- GERD or acid reflux
- Migraine headaches
- Anxiety or emotional stress
- Appendicitis (nausea with right lower abdominal pain)
- Gallbladder problems

ASSOCIATED SYMPTOMS:
- Stomach cramps and abdominal pain
- Diarrhea
- Fever
- Headache
- Dizziness
- Weakness and fatigue

WHEN TO SEEK EMERGENCY CARE:
Go to emergency room immediately if you have:
- Blood in vomit (looks red or like coffee grounds)
- Severe abdominal pain or cramping
- Signs of dehydration: dry mouth, no urination for 8 hours, extreme thirst, dizziness
- Vomiting after a head injury
- Vomiting with severe headache and stiff neck (possible meningitis)
- Vomiting with chest pain (possible heart attack)
- High fever above 103F with vomiting
- Cannot keep any liquids down for 12 hours (adults) or 4 hours (children)
- Confusion or altered consciousness

HOME CARE:
- Rest your stomach — do not eat solid foods until vomiting stops
- Take small sips of clear liquids every 15 minutes: water, broth, ginger ale, sports drinks
- Suck on ice chips if you cannot keep water down
- Gradually introduce bland foods: crackers, toast, rice, bananas, applesauce
- Avoid dairy, greasy, spicy, or sweet foods
- Avoid lying flat — prop yourself up slightly
- Ginger tea or ginger ale may help reduce nausea

DEHYDRATION WARNING SIGNS:
- Dark yellow or no urine
- Dry or sticky mouth and throat
- Feeling dizzy when standing up
- Crying without tears (children)
- Sunken eyes

RISK TIER: LOW — Most nausea and vomiting resolves with home care.
HIGH — vomiting with blood, chest pain, severe headache, or stiff neck is an emergency.
